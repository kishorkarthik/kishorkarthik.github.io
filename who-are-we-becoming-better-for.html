Who Are We Becoming Better For?

June 2026



One of the things about growing up is staying grounded while life keeps throwing bricks at you. To get ahead in life, I guess, it's not enough just to have the drive to become better. Because better itself is subject to relativity.

In your early twenties, you're unlikely to have a clear picture of what "better" actually looks like. More often than not, you're trying to figure it out as you go. Somehow, it becomes incredibly hard to be sure of your own journey, to be sure of what you were doing, this is the point where you will start to drift off.

When you are unsure of what you are doing, every decision feels riskier, and every mistake feels heavier. Risk and regret have a way of feeding into each other. Before long, you're no longer trying to move forward as much as you're trying to avoid getting hurt.

When people think about becoming better, they're often less motivated by the future than they are dissatisfied with the present.

Abraham Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs, explains that people are motivated by unmet needs. A need once satisfied stops being a need, and therefore stops being a source of motivation.

Maslow organized these needs into a hierarchy. At the bottom were the needs that allow us to survive. Above them came safety, then love and belonging, followed by esteem. At the very top was self-actualization, which is the desire to become what one is capable of becoming.

But reality is rarely that simple. People tend to have a variety of concurrent needs. More often than not, dissatisfaction comes not from a single unmet need, but from several of them competing for our attention. The result is not always failure. More often, it's drift. It's disorientation.

It`s a bit like space flight. In space flight, orientation refers to the direction your vehicle is pointing relative to the Sun, Earth, and other spacecraft. If you lose control of it, two things happen: the vehicle starts to tumble and spin, disorienting everything on board, and it also strays from its course. What begins as a subtle loss of direction becomes a full drift away from trajectory.

To stop the tumble in the vacuum of space, a pilot cannot simply fire the main thrusters. Firing the engines while spinning wildly only accelerates the chaos, burning precious fuel while pushing the spacecraft deeper into the dark. Instead, the pilot must first stop the tumble. They must ignore the vast, terrifying emptiness outside the window, look down at the instruments, and re-establish the spacecraft's orientation using stable reference points like the Sun and the Earth.

Life is not so different. When you're drifting, your own judgment can become part of the tumble. Before you can decide what to do next, you first need a stable reference to reorient yourself against—an anchor.

The internet offers a surprisingly simple definition. An anchor in life is the people, values, routines, or beliefs that provide stability, emotional grounding, and a sense of direction. It's a useful definition. But it leaves an important question unanswered: what makes one anchor more reliable than another?

A practical anchor has to do three things. It has to remain more stable than we are, remain available when we're at our worst, and continue to improve our decisions when our own judgment becomes unreliable. I think, in practice, when you're dealing with decision-making, an anchor should explain why you should do what you should do in the long term.

Ultimately, only you can decide what works best for you. My observation is that careers change. Five-year plans change. Values evolve. People change. But close relationships tend to remain relevant across the widest range of life's decisions. That's what makes them such a practical anchor.

Every model has outliers. Good models are judged by how well they explain the median case, not the outliers. This essay is about the median case. And for the median person, close relationships are probably the broadest and most reliable anchor for long-term decision-making. And that leads us to the final question,

who are we becoming better for?
